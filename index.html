Chapter 01

What is Venture Capital?

Venture capital is a form of private financing where investors provide money to early-stage companies in exchange for equity — a stake in the business — in the hope of generating extraordinary returns.

"Venture capital is the art of backing people, not just businesses — at the precise moment when both the risk and the opportunity are highest."

Unlike a bank loan — which must be repaid with interest regardless of how the business performs — VC investment is a bet on the future. The investor receives no guaranteed return. If the company fails, the money is lost. If it succeeds spectacularly, the returns can be extraordinary: 10x, 50x, or even 100x the original investment.

This is why venture capitalists look for companies with the potential to grow very large, very fast — often called "high growth potential." They are not interested in stable, predictable businesses. They are looking for the next Google, Airbnb, or Stripe.

Venture capital occupies a specific niche in the broader world of finance. While stock market investors buy shares in publicly listed companies, and private equity firms acquire mature businesses, venture capitalists operate at the earliest, riskiest stage — before a company has proven itself.

The VC model works on a portfolio basis. Because most early-stage startups fail, VCs expect many of their bets to go to zero. The entire model depends on a small number of outsized winners — the so-called "power law" — where one exceptional company generates returns large enough to cover the losses on everything else.

In exchange for their capital, VCs typically receive preferred stock, a seat on the board, and significant influence over major strategic decisions.

The Power Law in Practice

In a typical VC portfolio of 20 companies, roughly half will return nothing, a third will return modest amounts, and just 1–2 companies will generate the majority of the fund's total returns. Only 10x or more changes the math of the entire fund.

Chapter 02

The Ecosystem

Venture capital is not a single entity. It is a rich ecosystem of interconnected players, each with distinct roles, incentives, and relationships.

01 / 08

Limited Partners (LPs)

The ultimate source of capital. LPs invest money into VC funds and wait for returns — typically university endowments, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and family offices. LPs are "silent" — they provide capital but have no say in which companies are backed.

Capital Providers
02 / 08

General Partners (GPs)

The decision-makers. GPs raise the fund from LPs, source and evaluate opportunities, negotiate terms, sit on boards, and actively help portfolio companies grow. They earn a 2% management fee plus 20% carried interest on profits.

Fund Managers
03 / 08

Founders & Startups

The companies being funded. Founders exchange equity for VC investment, agreeing to share control and future profits in exchange for capital, expertise, and network that VCs provide. The founder-investor relationship can be transformative — or catastrophic.

Entrepreneurs
04 / 08

Angel Investors

Wealthy individuals investing their own money directly into very early-stage startups. Angels typically invest smaller amounts ($25K–$500K), move faster, and can be the critical first believers. Many successful founders become angels themselves.

Early Capital
05 / 08

Accelerators & Incubators

Structured programs investing small amounts into cohorts of early-stage startups in exchange for equity (typically 5–7%). Y Combinator has backed Airbnb, Dropbox, Stripe, Coinbase, and Reddit. Graduating from a top accelerator is often the fastest path to serious VC attention.

Launch Programs
06 / 08

Corporate VCs (CVCs)

Large companies operating their own venture arms — GV, Intel Capital, Salesforce Ventures, Amazon's Alexa Fund. CVCs invest strategically, seeking financial returns plus competitive intelligence. In 2024, CVCs participated in 46% of all global VC deal value.

Strategic Investors
07 / 08

Investment Banks & Advisors

Financial intermediaries facilitating late-stage transactions, IPOs, and M&A exits. Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and boutique advisors play a critical role in helping VC-backed companies go public or find strategic buyers.

Exit Facilitators
08 / 08

Lawyers & Law Firms

Deal counsel is essential at every stage. Specialised firms like Cooley, Gunderson, and Wilson Sonsini draft term sheets, investment agreements, and IP assignments. A good startup lawyer helps founders understand exactly what they're agreeing to.

Legal Counsel
Chapter 03

Funding Stages

Startups raise capital in stages, each corresponding to a specific phase of company development. Each round is larger than the last — and each comes with more scrutiny.

Pre-Seed
Idea Stage

The Very Beginning

The company may be little more than an idea, a prototype, or a founding team with a compelling hypothesis. No product, no customers, no revenue. Capital comes from founders, friends and family, or very early angels. Money is used to build the first version of a product and validate whether the core idea has merit.

$50K – $1M Equity given: 5–15%
Friends & Family Founders Angels
Seed
Validation Stage

Proving the Concept

The seed round funds initial product development and early go-to-market efforts. The team typically has a working MVP and perhaps early paying customers or strong user growth. SAFEs and convertible notes are commonly used to avoid setting a formal valuation early.

$500K – $4M Equity given: 10–25%
Seed Funds Angels YC / Techstars
Series A
Product-Market Fit

Scaling What Works

The first major institutional VC round. The company must demonstrate meaningful traction — recurring revenue (typically $1M–$5M ARR for SaaS), strong retention, and a credible path to scaling. Investors are buying into a business model, not just a vision. Board seats are assigned and the cap table is formalised.

$2M – $20M Equity given: 15–30%
VC Firms Seed-to-A Funds
Series B
Growth Stage

Scaling the Machine

Capital deployed to scale an already-proven model aggressively — expanding sales teams, entering new markets, building out operations. Revenue is typically in the tens of millions. Competition among investors for the best Series B deals is intense.

$15M – $60M Equity given: 10–20%
Growth VCs Multi-stage Funds
Series C+
Late Stage / Pre-IPO

The Run to Exit

Late-stage rounds fund the final phases of private growth before an exit. Companies often have hundreds of millions in revenue and valuations in the billions. The investor base broadens to sovereign wealth funds, hedge funds, and private equity firms. The exit — IPO or acquisition — is visible on the horizon.

Chapter 04

The Deal Process

From the first cold email to the wire transfer, a VC deal moves through a structured sequence of stages. Understanding each step demystifies a process that can otherwise feel opaque and intimidating.

01
Deal Sourcing
Ongoing · No fixed timeline

The process begins long before any pitch is given. VCs spend enormous energy building the pipelines that bring deals to them. The best deals rarely come from cold inbounds — they come from warm referrals, trusted networks, and proactive outreach. VC analysts monitor accelerator cohorts, research academic papers, attend conferences, and track fast-growing companies through PitchBook, Crunchbase, and LinkedIn signals.

A warm introduction from a trusted portfolio founder carries 10x the weight of a cold email, however polished.

Referral Networks Demo Days Cold Inbounds Proprietary Research Conference Sourcing
02
Initial Screening & Intro Call
Days 1–7 · 30–60 minute call

The first formal touchpoint is usually a brief introductory call. The VC makes a rapid assessment: Is this team exceptional? Is the market large enough? Is the product differentiated? Is there traction? Most deals are rejected at this stage. Your pitch deck should tell a compelling story — problem, solution, traction, market, team, and ask — in no more than 12–15 slides.

Deck Review Team Assessment Market Sizing Traction Check
03
Partner Meeting & Deep Dive
Weeks 1–3 · Multiple sessions

If the intro call goes well, you'll meet the full partnership. This is a much deeper conversation: 60–90 minutes or more across multiple sessions. Expect probing questions on unit economics, competitive moat, go-to-market strategy, and how you'll deploy capital. VCs test whether the founding team truly understands their own business — with clear-eyed awareness of the risks.

Full Partnership Pitch Unit Economics Deep Dive Technical Assessment Customer Discovery
04
Due Diligence
Weeks 2–6 · Most intensive phase

The VC firm sends a diligence request list covering four key areas: Financial — revenue model, growth rates, burn rate, projections. Legal — corporate structure, IP, employment contracts, existing agreements. Technical — technology stack, codebase quality, scalability. Commercial — customer, competitor, and industry expert reference calls to independently verify your claims.

Financial Audit Legal Review Technical Assessment Customer References Competitive Analysis
05
Investment Committee Approval
Week 4–5 · Internal review

An investment requires approval from the full partnership via an Investment Committee meeting. The champion partner presents the thesis, diligence findings, proposed valuation, and recommendation. Other partners ask hard questions and vote. This is where deals die silently — and why it's important to build relationships with multiple people at the firm, not just your single point of contact.

Partner Presentation Risk Assessment Portfolio Fit Consensus Vote
06
Term Sheet Negotiation
Week 5–7 · High-stakes negotiation

If the IC approves, the VC issues a term sheet — a non-binding document outlining proposed deal terms: pre-money valuation, investment amount, security type, liquidation preferences, anti-dilution provisions, pro-rata rights, board composition, and protective provisions. While valuation gets all the attention, protective provisions and liquidation preferences can matter far more at exit.

Pre-Money Valuation Liquidation Prefs Board Composition Pro-Rata Rights Anti-Dilution
07
Legal Documentation & Closing
Weeks 6–10 · Legal execution

Both sides' lawyers draft the full legal agreements: the Stock Purchase Agreement (SPA), the Investor Rights Agreement (IRA), the Voting Agreement, and the Right of First Refusal Agreement (ROFR). Once all documents are signed by all parties, the VC wires the funds. The deal is officially closed.

Stock Purchase Agreement Investor Rights Agreement Voting Agreement ROFR / Co-Sale Cap Table Update
08
Post-Investment Value-Add
Years 1–7 · Ongoing relationship

Closing the deal is the beginning of a long-term relationship. Good VCs work actively to help portfolio companies succeed: board oversight, network introductions, recruiting assistance, and operational expertise. The relationship culminates in an exit — an IPO or acquisition — that returns capital to LPs. The average VC fund holds investments for 7–10 years before most companies reach exit stage.

Board Governance Network Introductions Hiring Support Follow-on Rounds Exit Strategy

India VC: The Macro Tailwinds

Three structural forces underpin India's long-term VC opportunity: (1) Digital Public Infrastructure — UPI, Aadhaar, ONDC, and Account Aggregator frameworks create plug-and-play regulatory rails that dramatically reduce time-to-market for fintech and commerce startups. (2) Talent Arbitrage — India's 1.5M engineering graduates annually and $300B IT services industry produce world-class builders at globally competitive compensation. (3) Domestic Market Scale — 900M+ internet users approaching $4T GDP, with the next 300M consumers digitising in the next decade, creates a domestic addressable market rivalled only by China and the US.

Chapter 08

The Glossary

Venture capital has its own dense language. Master these terms and you'll be able to navigate any VC conversation, term sheet, or investor update with confidence.

Pre-Money Valuation
Deal Structure
The agreed value of a company before new investment is added. If a VC invests $5M at a $20M pre-money valuation, the company is worth $25M post-investment and the VC owns 20%.
Term Sheet
Deal Structure
A non-binding document (typically 5–15 pages) outlining the key terms of a proposed investment — valuation, amount, equity type, rights, and governance. Most term sheets include binding exclusivity and confidentiality clauses.
SAFE
Deal Structure
Simple Agreement for Future Equity. A one-page instrument invented by Y Combinator that gives investors the right to convert their investment into equity at the company's next priced round — usually at a discount or subject to a valuation cap.
Convertible Note
Deal Structure
A short-term loan that converts into equity at a future financing round. Unlike a SAFE, it accrues interest and has a maturity date. The investor typically receives a 15–25% discount or benefits from a valuation cap when converting.
Priced Round
Deal Structure
A financing round in which a formal valuation is set and new preferred shares are issued at a specific price per share. Priced rounds (typically Series A and beyond) involve full legal documentation and board changes.
Cap Table
Deal Structure
Capitalisation table — a spreadsheet detailing every shareholder's stake in the company, listing founders, employees (options and vested shares), angels, and institutional investors.
Liquidation Preference
Economics
A provision giving investors the right to receive their money back before founders and common shareholders receive anything in a sale or wind-down. A "1x non-participating" preference is the investor-friendly standard; "2x participating" is aggressive and founder-unfriendly.
Anti-Dilution
Economics
Protection for investors if the company later raises money at a lower valuation (a "down round"). Broad-based weighted average anti-dilution adjusts the conversion price proportionally. "Full ratchet" anti-dilution dramatically dilutes founders.
Dilution
Economics
The reduction in an existing shareholder's percentage ownership when new shares are issued. Every funding round dilutes earlier shareholders — founders, employees, and prior investors all own a smaller slice of a (hopefully) larger pie.
Option Pool
Economics
A reserved block of equity (typically 10–20%) set aside to grant stock options to future employees. The option pool is usually carved out before the pre-money valuation is set, meaning it dilutes the founders — not the new investors. This "option pool shuffle" is a key negotiating point founders often miss.
Pro-Rata Rights
Economics
The contractual right for an investor to participate in future funding rounds to maintain their percentage ownership. Highly coveted — especially for early investors in breakout companies — as they allow continued exposure to the upside.
Down Round
Economics
A funding round where a company raises capital at a lower valuation than its previous round. Down rounds signal deterioration in the company's prospects and typically trigger anti-dilution adjustments and significant dilution for founders.
Board of Directors
Governance
The governing body of a corporation, responsible for major strategic decisions, CEO oversight, and fiduciary duty to shareholders. In VC-backed companies, the board typically consists of founder seats, investor seats, and independent directors.
Drag-Along Rights
Governance
A provision allowing majority shareholders to force minority shareholders to approve — and participate in — a sale of the company. This prevents a small group from blocking a strategic acquisition that the majority and board have approved.
Tag-Along Rights
Governance
The right for minority shareholders to join a sale if majority shareholders are selling their stake. They protect small investors from being left behind when larger shareholders exit at favourable terms.
Protective Provisions
Governance
Veto rights granted to preferred stockholders over certain major company decisions — such as raising more capital, selling the company, or issuing new share classes. These ensure VCs have a meaningful check on decisions that could negatively affect their investment.
ARR / MRR
Metrics
Annual Recurring Revenue and Monthly Recurring Revenue — the bedrock metrics for SaaS businesses. VCs use ARR multiples (often 5–15x for fast-growing SaaS) to anchor valuations. Growth rate and predictability matter as much as the absolute number.
Burn Rate & Runway
Metrics
Burn rate is the net cash a company spends each month. Runway = Cash ÷ Monthly Net Burn. VCs expect founders to maintain 12–18+ months of runway at all times. Fundraising with less than 6 months dramatically reduces negotiating leverage.
LTV : CAC Ratio
Metrics
Lifetime Value to Customer Acquisition Cost. A ratio of 3:1 is the minimum healthy threshold; 5:1 is excellent. A low ratio means you're spending too much to acquire customers who don't stay long enough.
NRR (Net Revenue Retention)
Metrics
The percentage of revenue retained from existing customers, accounting for expansions, contractions, and churn. NRR above 100% means existing customers are spending more over time. NRR of 130%+ (seen in Snowflake, Datadog) means the company can grow even without acquiring new customers.
PMF (Product-Market Fit)
Metrics
The elusive state where a product genuinely solves a compelling problem for a well-defined market. Indicators include high retention, organic word-of-mouth growth, and customers who would be "very disappointed" without your product. Pre-PMF, all resources go to finding it; post-PMF, scale aggressively.
Management Fee
Fund Mechanics
The annual fee (typically 2% of committed capital) charged to LPs to cover the VC firm's operating expenses. On a $200M fund, this generates $4M/year. Real wealth for GPs comes from carried interest, not management fees.
Carried Interest ("Carry")
Fund Mechanics
The GP's share of the fund's profits — typically 20% — after returning invested capital to LPs. On a $200M fund returning $600M, LPs get $200M back first, then the $400M profit is split: $320M to LPs and $80M to GPs as carry.
Dry Powder
Fund Mechanics
The uninvested capital a fund has available to deploy. As of early 2025, US VC funds alone held $307.8B in dry powder — signalling significant pent-up investment demand ready to deploy as conditions improve.
Vintage Year
Fund Mechanics
The year a VC fund began investing. Vintage year is critically important for benchmarking performance — a fund deploying in 2009 vs. 2021 (peak valuations) experienced dramatically different market conditions and comparable returns.
IPO
Exits
Initial Public Offering — the process by which a private company lists its shares on a public stock exchange for the first time. The process involves months of preparation, SEC filings, a roadshow, and significant investment banking fees.
M&A (Acquisition)
Exits
Mergers & Acquisitions — when a company is purchased by another company or a private equity firm. M&A is the most common exit route in venture capital. Famous examples: Facebook acquiring Instagram for $1B (2012), Microsoft acquiring GitHub for $7.5B (2018).
DPI & TVPI
Exits
DPI (Distributions to Paid-In capital) measures actual cash returned to LPs vs. capital invested — the only metric that truly matters. TVPI adds unrealised paper value. A fund with 2x DPI has returned twice the invested capital in cash.
Unicorn / Decacorn
Exits
A unicorn is a private startup valued at $1B or more — a term coined by Aileen Lee in 2013. As of 2025, India alone has 118 unicorns. A decacorn is valued at $10B+; a hectocorn at $100B+.
Secondary Sale
Exits
The sale of existing shares by an investor or founder to a third party buyer, without the company raising new capital. Secondary markets (Forge, Nasdaq Private Market) have become an important liquidity mechanism for late-stage companies with IPO delays.
Chapter 09

How to Approach Venture Capital

Whether you're a founder raising your first round or an investor evaluating the industry, these principles distil the hard-won wisdom of thousands of deals.

What VCs Are Really Evaluating

Beneath the polished frameworks and financial models, venture capital is fundamentally a judgment business. VCs are making high-conviction bets on people, markets, and timing — often with limited information. Understanding what matters most can transform how you present your company.

The single most important thing is the quality of the founding team. VCs know that markets shift, products pivot, and strategies evolve — but exceptional founders figure it out. After team: a large and growing market, a product that customers genuinely love, and unit economics that suggest the business can scale profitably.

  • Build genuine relationships with investors before you need capital — warm introductions convert at 10x the rate of cold outreach
  • Know your numbers cold — burn rate, runway, MoM growth, LTV:CAC, NRR — and be ready to explain the story behind each metric
  • Target investors who have backed companies in your sector and stage — a specialist is worth far more than a generalist writing a cheque
  • Create competitive tension — running a tight, time-boxed process with multiple interested firms produces better outcomes than sequential approaches
  • Hire a startup lawyer before signing anything — protective provisions matter far more than valuation at exit
  • Do your own diligence on potential investors — reference calls with their portfolio founders are as important as the diligence they do on you

Do

  • Tell a clear, concise story in your pitch
  • Show genuine passion for the problem
  • Be honest about risks and unknowns
  • Demonstrate deep customer insight
  • Have a specific use of funds plan
  • Know your competitive landscape cold
  • Present a realistic path to exit
  • Have a strong reference network ready
  • Negotiate on terms, not just valuation
  • Maintain consistent investor communication

Don't

  • Claim you have "no competition"
  • Project hockey-stick growth without basis
  • Over-engineer the pitch deck design
  • Approach investors without a warm intro
  • Take the first term sheet without shopping
  • Agree to terms you don't understand
  • Focus solely on valuation, ignore terms
  • Delay fundraising until you're desperate
  • Give a board seat too early or cheaply
  • Confuse revenue with bookings or GMV

The VC Selection Framework

Not all money is equal. When choosing between investors, evaluate: (1) Relevant portfolio experience — have they built this type of company before? (2) Network quality in your sector. (3) Board behaviour — do founders speak highly post-investment? (4) Reserve capital — will they follow on in future rounds? (5) Fund stage and vintage — is there enough life left in the fund to support you to exit?
